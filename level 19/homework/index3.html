What is Shawarma?

Shawarma is a popular Middle Eastern dish made of thinly sliced cuts of seasoned meat, typically lamb, chicken, or beef, which is cooked on a vertical rotisserie. The meat is marinated with spices such as cumin, paprika, and garlic, then slow-cooked for hours. Once the meat is tender, it is sliced thinly and served with a variety of accompaniments, like fresh vegetables, sauces, and flatbreads, making it a flavorful and satisfying meal.

The History of Shawarma

Shawarma has ancient roots in the Middle East and is believed to have originated in Turkey, where it is called " döner kebab." The dish was later adapted in the Levant region (which includes countries like Lebanon, Syria, and Jordan), where it evolved into the version we know today. Shawarma gained popularity worldwide in the late 20th century as people from the Middle East moved to different parts of the world, bringing this delicious dish with them. It has since become a global street food, beloved by foodies everywhere.

Shawarma's Global Popularity

Today, Shawarma can be found in cities around the world, from New York to London to Cairo. The dish has been embraced in many cultures, with each place adding its own twist to the preparation and toppings. Shawarma restaurants are especially common in urban areas with large Middle Eastern communities, and food trucks offering Shawarma are often seen in busy downtown districts. The dish has become a quick and easy meal option for people on the go, thanks to its savory flavor and satisfying portion size.

Common Ingredients

  • Meat – Chicken, lamb, or beef (marinated with a blend of spices)
  • Spices – Cumin, paprika, turmeric, cinnamon, garlic, and more
  • Vegetables – Fresh tomatoes, lettuce, cucumbers, and onions
  • Flatbreads – Pita, laffa, or flatbreads to wrap the Shawarma
  • Sauces – Garlic sauce, tahini, yogurt sauce, and spicy chili sauce
